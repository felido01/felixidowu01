HR Employee Attrition Analytics

Uncovering patterns in employee turnover to improve retention using Power BI, Python, and Google Sheets.

View Resources Contact Me

Introduction

Employee attrition reflects workplace culture, satisfaction, and organizational health. High turnover increases costs, disrupts teams, and impacts morale. This project analyzes the IBM HR Employee Attrition dataset to uncover turnover patterns, using Power BI to deliver actionable insights for better retention.

Project Overview

This project identifies key factors driving employee attrition, providing HR teams with insights to enhance retention. Using Power BI for visualizations and Python for analysis, it showcases my ability to transform data into strategic recommendations.

Key Objectives

  • Identify primary factors contributing to employee attrition.
  • Provide actionable insights to improve retention rates.
  • Analyze attrition patterns by age, department, job role, and satisfaction.
  • Visualize key metrics through an interactive Power BI dashboard.

Dataset Description

The IBM HR Analytics Employee Attrition dataset from Kaggle includes comprehensive employee data, such as age, department, job role, satisfaction, and attrition status, enabling detailed analysis of turnover factors.

  • Age: Employee’s age.
  • Department: Department of employment (e.g., Research & Development).
  • Job Role: Specific role (e.g., Laboratory Technician).
  • Job Satisfaction: Employee satisfaction level.
  • Monthly Income: Employee’s monthly salary.
  • Attrition: Whether the employee left (Yes/No).
  • Gender: Employee’s gender.
  • Job Level: Employee’s job level within the organization.

Access the dataset on Kaggle.

Data Cleaning Process

The dataset was cleaned to ensure accuracy and usability for analysis, using Google Sheets and Python.

  • Google Sheets: Used Find and Replace to decode values and formatted currency columns for consistency.
  • Python: Imported data into Pandas, performed EDA with .shape, .head(), .info(), and .describe(), and checked for missing or duplicate values (none found).
  • Visualization: Created quick plots with Matplotlib and Seaborn to assess attrition counts.

View the prepared dataset: Download.

Dashboard Design Process

A Power BI dashboard was created to visualize attrition factors, enhanced with a custom Figma background for aesthetic appeal. Power Query and DAX were used for data transformation and metric calculations.

  • Figma: Designed a custom dashboard background for visual consistency.
  • Power Query: Transformed data for analysis readiness.
  • DAX: Calculated metrics like total attrition, employee count, and gender distribution.
  • Charts: Developed visualizations for attrition by age, department, job role, and satisfaction.

Key Insights & Observations

The analysis revealed a 16.12% attrition rate and key factors driving turnover.

Age & Job Level

Younger employees and Job Level 1 staff show higher attrition rates.

Department

Research & Development has the highest attrition, especially among Laboratory Technicians.

Job Satisfaction

Low satisfaction correlates with high attrition; high satisfaction also shows unexpected turnover.

Gender

Male employees exhibit higher attrition than female employees.

Compensation

Employees earning $1,000–$5,000/month have the highest attrition rates.

Recommendations

Strategic actions to reduce attrition and improve retention include:

  • Young Employees & Job Level 1: Implement mentorship, training, and clear career paths.
  • Research & Development: Conduct surveys and exit interviews to address department-specific issues.
  • Laboratory Technicians: Offer job rotation and development opportunities.
  • Job Satisfaction: Regularly measure satisfaction and address workload or growth concerns.
  • Male Employees: Investigate gender-specific turnover through targeted surveys.
  • Compensation: Review salaries for employees earning $1,000–$5,000/month and add incentives.

Challenges Encountered

Implementing retention strategies may face obstacles, including:

  • Resistance to Participation: Employees or managers may resist new initiatives.
  • Resource Constraints: Limited time and budget may hinder program development.
  • Low Survey Participation: Inaccurate or insufficient data could skew insights.

Conclusion

This project highlights key attrition factors and offers targeted strategies to enhance retention. By addressing age, department, satisfaction, and compensation issues, organizations can build a more engaged workforce.
